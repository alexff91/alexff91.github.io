The Grounding Trap

How we built a document-routed RAG system for the Agentic Legal RAG Challenge, why citing too many pages destroyed our score, and what the F-beta formula actually means when your gold standard is three pages.

01The Competition

The Agentic RAG Legal Challenge is an international competition at Dubai AI Week. Build a system that answers legal questions grounded in DIFC documents — court decisions, laws, regulations. 900 questions. 298 documents. 4,038 pages. Two submission attempts.

The scoring formula makes optimization interesting:

Total = (0.7 × S_det + 0.3 × S_asst) × G × T × F

Every factor is multiplicative. A weak grounding score doesn't subtract — it divides. This matters more than anything else in the paper.

02Architecture

Every ARLC question names specific documents: "What is the claim amount in CFI 035/2025?" So we skip vector search entirely for 96% of questions. Regex extracts the reference, a lookup table maps it to the document hash, and the LLM gets the full document.

Document Router

Five regex families: case references (CFI/SCT/CA/ARB), law names (25+ patterns), regulations, orders, and court procedure keywords. 864 of 900 questions route to specific documents. Zero API calls. Zero latency.

04The Grounding Trap

The mistake: F-beta with β=2.5 weights recall 6.25x over precision. We read that as "cite more pages." But when gold is ~3 pages, citing 13 gives F=0.66 and citing 42 gives F=0.36. We learned this after using both submissions.

05Lessons

1. Do the math first

We spent two weeks on answer quality and lost 40% of the score to a grounding strategy we chose without computing F-beta sensitivity. One spreadsheet calculation would have changed everything.

2. Routing beats retrieval

For domain-specific corpora where questions name documents, regex routing outperforms embedding similarity. 96% coverage, zero hallucination risk, zero cost.

3. Let the LLM cite pages

When the model sees the full document, it can tell you which pages matter. That signal is more precise than any retrieval heuristic. Trust it.

4. Submission budget is everything

Two attempts for the final phase. We used both on the same flawed strategy and discovered the fix an hour too late. Validate your weakest metric first.

5. What didn't help

  • LLM page reranking: added latency, slightly hurt grounding
  • Self-consistency voting: +15% accuracy but 3x slower
  • Embedding search for routed questions: unnecessary noise
  • Cross-reference expansion: added non-gold pages

06What I'd change

  1. Start with the F-beta spreadsheet, not the pipeline
  2. Validate grounding strategy during warmup, not just answer quality
  3. Cite only LLM-reported pages — no expansion heuristics
  4. Build a local evaluation harness to iterate offline
  5. Save at least one submission for a fundamentally different approach

07Conclusion

Document routing + full-document context works. 96% coverage, strong answers, perfect TTFT. The system's weakness was a single design choice: how many pages to cite. We chose 13 when the answer was 5.

Post-competition analysis: with 4.2 pages average, our score would have been ~0.68 instead of 0.435. A 57% improvement from one number.

In multiplicative scoring, every factor is a gate. Optimize the weakest one first.
