The Grounding Trap: Building a Legal RAG System for ARLC 2026

How we built a document-routed RAG pipeline for the Agentic RAG Legal Challenge at Dubai AI Week — 96% routing coverage, dual-model LLM strategy, and the F-beta math mistake that cost us 40% of our score.

Abstract

We describe our approach to the Agentic RAG Legal Challenge (ARLC) 2026, a benchmark competition for production-grade AI systems in the legal domain. Our system combines regex-based document routing (96% coverage) with full-document LLM context, a hybrid retrieval stack (TF-IDF + BM25 + text-embedding-3-large with Reciprocal Rank Fusion), and a dual-model LLM strategy (GPT-5.4-mini for deterministic types, GPT-5.2 for free-text).

Our key finding: grounding precision dominates the scoring function — even with F-beta β=2.5 favoring recall, over-citing pages by 4x destroys the score. We document this "grounding trap" and other lessons for practitioners building legal RAG systems.

1. The Competition

ARLC 2026 is an international competition at Dubai AI Week evaluating AI systems on legal question answering over DIFC (Dubai International Financial Centre) documents — court decisions, laws, regulations, and practice directions.

The scoring formula creates interesting optimization dynamics:

Total = (0.7 × S_det + 0.3 × S_asst) × G × T × F

Where S_det is deterministic accuracy, S_asst is free-text quality, G is grounding (F-beta β=2.5), T is TTFT multiplier, and F is format compliance. The multiplicative structure means each factor is a gate — a low grounding score destroys the total regardless of answer quality.

2. System Architecture

Our key insight: every ARLC question references specific documents by case number or law name. This means document routing is more effective than general retrieval — if you can map "CFI 035/2025" to a specific document hash, you eliminate retrieval noise entirely.

Document Router (96% Coverage)

The router extracts structured references using five families of regex patterns:

  • Case references: CFI, SCT, CA, ARB, TCD, DEC, ENF with various formatting
  • Law names: 25+ patterns ("DIFC Law No. 1 of 2004", "Employment Law", etc.)
  • Regulations & Orders: Order No., DRA Order, Cabinet Resolution
  • Court procedure keywords: Fallback routing to Rules of Court

Result: 864/900 questions (96%) mapped directly to source documents. Zero API calls. Zero latency. Zero hallucination risk.

Hybrid Retrieval (Fallback)

For the 4% of unrouted questions, we use:

  • TF-IDF: Trigram features (80K), legal-aware tokenizer preserving "Article 8(1)"
  • BM25: k1=1.5, b=0.75
  • Embeddings: text-embedding-3-large (3,072 dims)
  • Fusion: Reciprocal Rank Fusion (k=60)
  • SAC: Summary-Augmented Chunking to reduce retrieval mismatch

4. The Grounding Trap

Key Insight

5. Lessons Learned

Lesson 1: Run the Math First

We spent 2 weeks optimizing answer quality, then lost 40% of our score to a grounding strategy chosen without computing F-beta sensitivity. Always calculate metric impact before building.

Lesson 2: Simple Routing Beats Complex Retrieval

For domain-specific corpora where questions reference documents by name, regex routing is more reliable than embedding similarity. Our router achieved 96% coverage with zero API calls, zero latency, and zero hallucination risk. Before building a retrieval stack, check if pattern matching can route most queries.

Lesson 3: Full-Doc Context Enables LLM Self-Citation

When the LLM sees the entire document, it can accurately self-report which pages contain the answer. This is more reliable than any retrieval heuristic. For documents ≤50 pages, send full text and ask the LLM to report page numbers.

Lesson 4: Submission Budget is Precious

With only 2 final submissions, there's no room for iterative optimization. Both our submissions used variations of the same over-citation strategy. We discovered the optimal approach after exhausting our budget. Use warmup phases to validate your weakest metric.

Lesson 5: What Didn't Help

  • LLM page reranking: Added latency, slightly hurt grounding (−0.012)
  • Self-consistency voting: +12–18% accuracy but 3x latency — poor tradeoff
  • Embedding retrieval for routed questions: Unnecessary noise when target is known
  • Cross-reference page expansion: Added pages not in gold standard

6. What We Would Do Differently

  1. Start with grounding math. Calculate F-beta for different citation counts before building anything.
  2. Validate grounding in warmup. Our 15 warmup submissions focused on answers, not citations.
  3. Cite only LLM-reported pages. No expansion, no heuristics. Trust the LLM's page identification.
  4. Use structured outputs (json_schema) for guaranteed valid JSON.
  5. Build a local evaluation harness to test grounding without burning submissions.
  6. Reserve final submissions — make each test a fundamentally different hypothesis.

7. Conclusion

Our ARLC 2026 entry demonstrates that document routing + full-document context is a viable architecture for legal RAG. With 96% routing coverage and strong answers (Det=0.840), our weakness was an over-citation strategy that inflated pages from the optimal ~5 to ~13, costing ~40% of our score.

Post-competition analysis with optimized citation (avg 4.2 pages) estimates our score would have been 0.65–0.68 — a 57% improvement from a single parameter change.

The key takeaway: in multiplicative scoring systems, every component is a gate. Optimize the weakest factor first, and always run the math before choosing a strategy.
